CHINA GP 2026 – Race Analysis

Arnau Gàndara Miranda

The 2026 Chinese Grand Prix, held at the Shanghai International Circuit, delivered a race that provided some early conclusions about the teams in this new chapter of the championship, further highlighting Mercedes' dominance over the rest of the grid. Conditions were ideal for racing: a dry track, with ambient temperatures of 15 °C and track temperatures around 23 °C. The starting grid was led by Andrea Kimi Antonelli, followed by George Russell, Lewis Hamilton, Charles Leclerc and Oscar Piastri. All front-row starters opted for medium tyres, choosing a flexible strategy.

The start was one of the most eventful moments. The Ferrari drivers, Hamilton and Leclerc, delivered an impeccable getaway, gaining several positions (Hamilton gained two, moving from 3rd to 1st, and Leclerc moved from 4th to 3rd), proving that their starting performance remains one of their greatest strengths. Other teams, however, suffered more: contact between the two Cadillac cars dropped them to the back of the field; Isack Hadjar slid while defending his position, forcing Oliver Bearman off track; and both McLarens failed to even take the start, significantly disrupting the race's development.

During the first stint, the medium tyres surprised with their consistent performance. Degradation only became noticeable around laps 12-14, around the same time that Mercedes' engine dominance began to show. Antonelli and Russell maintained a very steady pace, especially on the circuit's long straights, where their engine power allowed them to pull away lap after lap. The first pit stops occurred between laps 16 and 18, with most drivers switching to hard tyres in an attempt to complete a one-stop strategy (the pre-race favourite). Everyone followed the same pattern, indicating that the race would be decided by pure pace rather than strategic ingenuity. After the stops, Mercedes' leadership solidified: Antonelli began to pull away with an unmatched rhythm, while Russell comfortably held second place, building a gap of up to 20 seconds over Hamilton in third.

One of the most remarkable data points from the Grand Prix was the pace difference between the Mercedes drivers and the rest of the field. Both Antonelli and Russell lapped consistently around 1:37.2, while the Ferraris were stuck in the 1:37.5-1:37.6 range. This difference of three or four tenths per lap, seemingly minimal, translates into an advantage of over fifteen seconds over a fifty-lap race. This demonstrates Mercedes' superiority in top speed and acceleration, particularly exiting slow-speed corners, a crucial trait at this circuit.

Nevertheless, Ferrari has found a clear strength in race starts, with Hamilton securing his first podium for the Italian team. In contrast, teams like Red Bull Racing and McLaren are still adapting to the new regulations and have yet to show their usual competitive level, as evidenced by McLaren's failure to even start the race due to engine issues.

The final stint of the race was quiet, with no major battles for the lead. Antonelli managed the pace perfectly on his way to the chequered flag, seeing it first for the first time in his career, making him the second-youngest driver to achieve this feat. Hamilton completed the podium, followed by a strong Leclerc, who capitalized on Ferrari's early-race strengths. In summary, the Chinese Grand Prix made it clear that Mercedes currently dominates thanks to their engine power and driver consistency, while Ferrari excels at starts and does everything possible to keep the fight alive. This race also highlights the gap between those who have understood the new regulations and those still struggling to adapt to the change.
